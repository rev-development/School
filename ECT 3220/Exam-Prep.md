### From Page to Screen: How Digital Media Is Reinventing Storytelling

"They’re introducing nonlinear narratives, episodic formats, and even user-driven plots that invite audiences to co-create their experiences."

Streaming services do more than allow people to watch the shows they want, when they want them. They allow people choice over their media intake and that has grown into a form of audience interaction. Shows can cater to a specific demographic instead of needing to appease everyone. Now that has evolved into shows being selectively episodic or segmented with different endings. Content hunger has also increased, people binge shows now, they want a story from start to finish.

"Streaming platforms allow creators to craft layered arcs, exploring subplots and character backstories in a way that would be impractical in traditional formats."

Shows don't need to anticipate being picked up by the un-initiated, they can live in niches.

## Short Idea

**Thesis**

Generative AI will vastly change how cinematic VR experiences are created by giving individuals unprecedented creative agency. And will eventually lead to a period where we've "killed our writers" before causing a recursion to more traditional forms.

**The Writer's Challenge**

One of the major challenges presented in creating cinematic VR experiences is that you simultaneously need to direct a viewer's attention to the story you want to tell while leveraging the medium to allow that viewer to see the story they want to hear. In "Modern Storytelling: Embracing New Techniques in a Digital Era - Eye On Films", Dimitri said "Through immersive environments, audiences can now interact with stories in unprecedented ways, experiencing narratives firsthand rather than passively consuming them." (2025) Two major strategies for doing this are through 'complete immersion' and 'shiny keys with easter eggs'.

'Complete immersion' being the tool of recreated historical experiences where the problem of negative space is solved by leaving it as negative space because that is authentic to the experience. If someone wants to stare at their feet during Pompeii, then they experience what its like to stare at their feet during Pompeii. The second approach is used by most cinematic VR experiences, where there is the idealized attention path set forth by the creator and a viewer is kept close to that path through a series of exploitations of innate human behavior while not strictly punishing a viewer, through content loss, by including auxillary elements outside of the ideal attention path. This idea is expressed in "How VR Is Reshaping Interactive Storytelling" by Taylor where they said: "[sic] it presents challenges for writers. Instead of crafting a single plot, they must design multiple paths while ensuring each one remains compelling." (2025)

**The Game Changer**

Generative AI, however, provides a new avenue for creators. Instead of putting onus on the creator to devise an idealized attention path, it can create elements in the viewer's attention path. Generative AI has rebirthed the CYOA genre and there is an ongoing emergence of AI characters orchestrated by an AI 'narrator'. Whereas in traditional storytelling, a writer creates character with static dialogue to tell a narrative. Whether or not there are branches or choice, it is always a series of set destinations. "AI-powered personalization is shifting storytelling from something static to something dynamic, adapting based on audience choices, engagement levels, or emotional responses." (Heyob, 2025)

With generative AI, writers can now create the storyteller, not just the characters. This is a profound and landscape-changing development because it puts writers in the position where they have to essentially write either themselves, the laws of reality for an entire world, or a god to drive their experience. Once generative AI crosses the threshold to where it can insert a live character and generate appropriate visuals for a world based on user interaction with AI, the problem of negative space in cinematic VR can be 'solved'.

**Everybody is an Author**

And based on the evolution of user-driven experiences, soon those tools will be put in the hands of average people. Jenkins discusses this in "Digital Storytelling Evolution: Tracing Its Progress and Impact", but over time audiences have demonstrated a desire for more agency over the stories they experience. (2024)

There will be a raging fad where everyone is creating their own generative AI experiences, potentially sharing them if they are bold, but mostly creating their own narrative echo chambers. And we've seen this before, in Marlowe's article "From Page to Screen: How Digital Media Is Reinventing Storytelling" they said of digital media providers: "They’re introducing nonlinear narratives, episodic formats, and even user-driven plots that invite audiences to co-create their experiences." (2025)

**Everybody is a shitty Author**

The reason I predict this to be a fad though, is because it is susceptible to the same fault that birthed cinema, literature, and all story-sharing founded industries: Not everyone is good at it. At some point, media taste will likely circle back to more traditional forms because most people will exhaust their imaginations. If everyone was able to self-entertain with thrilling narratives imagined on the fly, then why would we ever buy a movie ticket?
